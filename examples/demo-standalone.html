T H E   H I G H - S I G N A L   T A K E A W A Y S
Eight rules worth keeping.

Transferable decision rules pulled straight from the conversation. Most survive even if you never touch a prop firm.

01 The edge is the profit model, not the strategy. Most traders chase alpha; the real goal is money. Engineer positive EV around the rules and a slight edge prints.
02 Optimise to the rule set, not the market. For any win rate and risk-reward, one firm's rules fit best. Simulate across them; trade the fit.
03 Eval and funded are different problems. Eval: maximise P(target before max loss). Funded: maximise P(payout) × size. He runs different strategies on each.
04 Make everything static. Fixed risk, fixed stop, fixed target — the only live decision is the entry. On a firm, static beats "technical" levels.
05 Match risk-reward to the drawdown. A $3k target vs a $2k trailing drawdown is ~1:1.5 — trade that and pass more.
06 Volume, not conviction. 20 trades a day at a slight edge compresses a month into a session — and turns ~98% of days green by sample size alone.
07 Risk of ruin before returns. Size accounts so ruin is a few percent or less. He went from a 5% to a 0.5% risk of ruin.
08 Only trade firms that pay. He has had ~$300k in payouts denied. Test a new firm with one account; scale in only once it pays.

T H E   M E N T A L   M O D E L
How he frames the game.

Before any tactic, JJ reframes what a prop firm even is — and that single reframe is where the whole method comes from.

THE HOUSE HAS A BAKED EDGE

A prop firm is a casino. The rules are structured so the most common live-trading approach does not work — that is how the house keeps its edge. If you trade a firm like a live account, you lose by design. They profit when the trader fails the eval. The eval fee is the cost of a ticket. Trailing drawdown and consistency rules are the house edge. Each funded payout is the casino paying out. You win by playing the odds at volume, not by one big hand. So the normal playbook is the losing playbook.

BE THE CARD COUNTER

The winning gambler does not fight the house — he counts cards and presses small, repeatable edges until thrown out. JJ does the same: a slight bias, sized to the rules, played as many times as possible.

THE SPLIT THAT RUNS EVERYTHING

One account type, two objectives.

Objective: hit the profit target before the max loss.

The profit model.

JJ does not start with a chart. He starts with the math that tells him whether the whole game is worth playing. Build it in this order — the entry comes last.

THE EXPECTED-VALUE ENGINE — SIX STEPS

01 Evaluation cost & pass rate — Two inputs run the eval phase: what an evaluation costs, and the share you pass. Track over at least 20–50 attempts.
02 Average cost to funded — His own term: if three evals cost $100 each and you pass one, your average cost to get one funded account is $300.
03 Payout probability × size — On the funded side, upside is P(payout) × payout size. He aims for ~30% pass rate and ~30% payout rate.
04 The EV ratio — Upside ÷ downside. He targets 3× return or better on evaluation spend. Below the base rate, do not play.
05 Risk of ruin → bankroll — With a ~5% payout chance, failing 20 in a row is 0.95²⁰. Size the bankroll so risk of ruin is a few percent or less.
06 Then, the entry — Only now does strategy matter. The model is the edge; the entry is the rounding.
WORKED EXAMPLE — A $10K MONTH
33% pass × 33% payout = one $2,000 payout per nine evals. Nine evals at ~$100 cost $900 to win $2,000 — a 2× return. Repeat ten times.
IF THE EV IS NOT THERE
Then there is nothing to trade. A great entry on a bad rule set still loses. Find the firm whose rules fit your stats before you place a single trade.
